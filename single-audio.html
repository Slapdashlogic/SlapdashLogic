Blue

  • May 13, 2018
  • MusicLyrical Discussion

Never seen a bluer sky. Yeah, I can feel it reaching out and moving closer. There's something about blue. Asked myself what it's all for you know the funny thing about it I couldn't answer No, I couldn't answer.

This is, according to creator the most profound song that was ever composed for the Cowboy Bebop series. It describes a first person the death of the character and their ascension towards the blue sky (that is, towards a better place). It gives one truly powerful and beautiful impression of death. This song makes invokes feelings of a special kind of awe, by dismantling how strong lyrical poetry can transmit into a listeners imagination... The listener can imagine what it feels like to be dying, losing consciousness, and ascending to the blue skies, then into black space. How great it would be to feel this type of release, it feels as if the listener is waking from a dream... And how awful at the same time, as the dream would be ending... We were wondering what the lyrics of the choirs might be, and didn't find any right answer. But we think they could be Gabriela Robin/Yoko Kanno's imaginary language, and it seems to be some kind of mixing between Japanese, portuguese, english, german, and maybe latin... It could indeed be Kanno's own translation of "Never seen a bluer sky" in her own "hanamogera" (that's how she calls that imaginary language). On the live version of the song (from the "Souvenir of Tokyo" video), the choir pronunciation is slightly different, but that might also just be the japanese accent of the choir singer replacing Gabriela Robin. We wonder what Kanno wrote as lyrics, for this substitute singer... Maybe she just told her orally. Here is what we hear (in romaji) : "Ue ju me sa ya unt du mye di kan tus".
